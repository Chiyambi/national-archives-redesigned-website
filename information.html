National Archives of Malawi — Safeguarding the Nation's Memory

History of the National Archives

A Wealth of Information

The National Archives of Malawi preserves and provides access to the nation's documentary heritage. This article outlines the remarkable history, holdings, and diverse activities of this vital institution.

Early Development

The early development of archival services in the then British Central African Protectorate was largely due to the efforts of British ministers who recognized the importance of preserving official records. In March 1914, Lord Harcourt, the Secretary of State for the Colonies, asked colonial governors to report on their arrangements for preserving official records.

In 1929, L. S. Amery demanded to be informed about both the condition and organization of records. His successor, W. O Ormsby Gore, emphasized that:

"The preservation of its records in a satisfactory state must be regarded as one of the first duties of a colonial government, a duty which derives greater urgency from... delay in the institution of suitable protective measures may and does lead to the inevitable loss of documents of value."

Establishment and Development

Because of such bold measures, a National Archives was established in Salisbury, Southern Rhodesia. Through a series of laws, this institution collected and preserved the records of the Federation of Rhodesia and Nyasaland.

In 1947, depots were opened in Livingstone (Northern Rhodesia) and in Zomba (Nyasaland). Eleven years later, a consolidating legislation was carefully drawn up, and in 1959, the Federal National Archives Act was passed. This act provided for the preservation, custody, control, and disposal of public and judicial records of the federation and its territories.

Independence and Transformation

Early in the 1960s, Nyasaland seceded from the Federation. In 1963, she became a self-governing state. On the dissolution of the federation, the Federal National Archives Act became obsolete and was amended through the Archives Enactment Regulation.

An Archives Working Party was established to make recommendations on how archival resources could be proportionally shared. This committee, later known as the Committee on Federal Records, consisted of a chairman, secretary, and representatives from each territory. For a decade (1964-74), the committee physically examined federal records and drew up disposal schedules.

What had been the Zomba depot of the Federal National Archives graduated into the National Archives of Malawi on January 1, 1964—just six months before Nyasaland became an independent state. To this day, the National Archives of Malawi operates as a department enforcing two acts of Parliament: the National Archives Act and the Printed Publications Act, through five professional divisions.

Holdings & Collections

From its humble beginning at independence, the National Archives of Malawi has grown to become one of the well-organized archival institutions in Africa. The growth of holdings has been remarkable:

1964: Foundation Year

4,500 cubic feet of government records

1984: Two Decades Later

39,000 cubic feet of government records

Present Day

60,000+ cubic feet of government records

Searchable Files

25,000+ files available in the search room

Manuscript Collections

The Historical Manuscript section maintains 174 accessions from various sources, including private institutions, churches, clubs, societies, and individuals who played roles in the nation's development.

Library Collection

The library has experienced unprecedented growth, expanding from just 120 books in 1964 to over 50,000 volumes today. This includes the Central Africana Collection and 1,495 periodical titles.

Divisions & Activities

  • 1. Records Management Centers

    There are three centers—one in each region—promoting government records management services. They serve as storage areas for non-current records and conduct records management surveys. These centers ensure proper transfer, appraisal, and disposition of records based on standing instructions developed jointly with government ministries and departments.

  • 2. Public Archives

    Records from centers reaching 40 years after file closure are appraised for historical value. Those deemed significant become Public Archives, no longer property of the creating department. Following International Council on Archives (ICA) standards, staff prepare finding aids and arrange access for researchers following government clearance procedures.

  • 3. Historical Manuscripts

    Collections include papers from institutions and individuals who shaped the nation's development. Services include collecting, arranging, describing collections through catalogs and guides, controlling the search room, providing advisory services, training private sector personnel, conducting business archives surveys, and coordinating oral history collection of prominent people.

  • 4. The Library

    Maintains comprehensive printed literature collections relating to Malawi and neighboring countries. Services include acquiring publications through purchase, legal deposit, gifts and exchange; providing reference and bibliographic services; compiling the Malawi National Bibliography; and acting as the national coordinating agency for the International Standard Book Numbering (ISBN) system. Researchers may consult materials without prior arrangement.

  • 5. Conservation

    Performs three critical functions: document repairing (extending useful life), book binding (protecting loose material), and microfilming (protecting at-risk material and creating multiple copies for security and access). These services ensure long-term preservation of the nation's documentary heritage.

Conclusion

The National Archives of Malawi stands as a vital institution safeguarding the nation's documentary and political heritage. Through its comprehensive holdings, professional divisions, and dedicated services, it ensures that Malawi's records of enduring value are preserved, organized, and made accessible to researchers, government officials, and the general public.

The Archives represents more than a repository—it is the custodian of the nation's memory, serving generations past, present, and future by preserving the documents that tell Malawi's story.
